Journal of Mammalogy Volume 1 described 4 new genera, 10 new species, and 8 new subspecies.

During the early part of the twentieth century, the technology to analyze genetics and create a cladogram showing the relationships between organism populations had not yet been developed. New descriptions were generally based upon perceived identifiable diagnostic differences between populations, usually morphological in nature.

As the science of phylogenetic systematics (commonly referred to as cladistics) advanced, some diagnostic differences have stood the test of time while others have been shown to be the result of natural selection pressures specific to the population but are not indicative of a distinct evolutionary lineage. In most cases when a described taxon name is invalidated, it is due to a consensus that the name is a junior synonym of a previously described taxon.

It should be noted that there is no such thing as an “official” listing or registry of valid taxon nomenclatures. Some organizations publish lists, but even respected scientists within those organizations usually have some disagreements with the list.

Personally speaking, when a taxon has an ITIS Taxonomical Serial Number (TSN), I use that system to determine if a taxon nomenclature should be considered valid unless I am aware of compelling research (usually recent) that disagrees with the ITIS report.

This listing attempts to identify the general scientific consensus on how the new taxons described in the Journal of Mammalogy Volume 1 have stood the test of time. This listing should not be considered authoritative.

Castor canadensis missouriensis

Described as a new subspecies of Castor canadensis by Vernon Bailey on page 32.

Hall and Kelson (1959) recognized this subspecies, along with 23 others for a total of 24. In his second edition, Hall (1981) also recognized this subspecies and the majority of the defined subspecies.

As of April 27, 2020, ITIS does not recognize any subspecies and lists Castor canadensis missouriensis [TSN 900118] as a junior synonym of Castor canadensis.

Like the Gray Wolf (Canis lupus), the North American Beaver is a classic example of the ‘Publish or Perish’ trend that resulted in an overabundance of subspecies descriptions during the first half of the twentieth century. Many subspecies were described, often with only superficial morphological differences justifying the description. Some of the subspecies descriptions may be valid, but the majority of them to me anyway read like they could simply be regional natural selection pressures rather than indicative of distinct lineages within the species.

To the best of my knowledge, a genetic review of the clades within Castor canadensis has not taken place. If and when that happens, it is possible some of the formerly described subspecies may be revived.

With no subspecies of Castor canadensis currently recognized, Castor canadensis is a monotypic species. As such, Castor canadensis missouriensis is currently viewed as an invalid junior synonym of Castor canadensis [Kuhl, 1820; TSN 180212].

Cavia tschudii arequipæ

Wilfred H. Osgood on page 34 assigned a new taxonomic name for this subspecies in order to avoid a naming clash.

As of April 28, 2020 the name Osgood assigned is still considered valid by the ITIS although it is now spelled without the ‘ae’ ligature as Cavia tschudii arequipae [Osgood, 1919; TSN 825520].

Micraonyx

Described as a new genus of Viverridae by J. A. Allen on page 24.

For his type specimen, Allen used Lutra leptonyx.

The species had already been proposed as a type species for the genus Leptonyx but as explained in Allen’s article, the genus name was unavailable, hence why he proposed Micraonyx.

I was not able to find any recent literature that accepted this new genus.

On April 25, 2020 the ITIS did not have a TSN for this genus, and the IBIS Mammal Database lists both Micraonyx and Leptonyx as junior synonyms as Aonyx.

It appears that the current general consensus of the scientific community is that Micraonyx is an invalid junior synonym of Aonyx [Lesson, 1827; TSN 621802].

Myotis albicinctus

Described as a new species of bat by Glover M. Allen on page 2.

In his species description, Allen notes that this bat is very similar to Myotis lucifugus altipetens and that both Dr. and Mrs. Joseph Grinnell were inclined to classify the skins as M. l. altipetens.

This taxon was recognized by Miller (1923 page 68).

I was not however able to find an academic reference either in the twenty-first century or in the latter half of the twentieth century that accepted Myotis albicinctus as valid let alone any that argued for its validity. The ITIS does not have a TSN for this nomenclature.

In Grinnel (1933 pages 85–86), both the taxon M. l. altipetens and the taxon M. albicinctus are identified as junior synonyms of M. l. carissima.

It is fairly safe to assume that the overwhelming consensus is that that Myotis albicinctus is an invalid junior synonym of Myotis lucifugus carissima [Thomas, 1904; TSN 948587].

Neofiber alleni nigrescens

Described as a new subspecies of Neofiber alleni [True, 1884] by Arthur H. Howell on page 79.

Rausch (1952) used this as a valid taxon.

Whitaker and Hamilton (1998) recommend this subspecies be treated as a synonym of Neofiber alleni and do not recognize any subspecies of Neofiber alleni.

The ITIS TSN for Neofiber alleni nigrescens is 972992 and as checked on April 22, 2020 the ITIS lists this nomenclature as an invalid junior synonym of Neofiber alleni [True, 1884; TSN 180357].

Osbornictis

Described as a new genus of Viverridae by J. A. Allen on page 25 where he notes it is closely related to Genetta.

The genus was described for the purpose of a new species he was describing, Osbornictis piscivora.

The genus remained monotypic containing only that species and very little natural history about the genus could be determined as specimens belonging to that species proved to be very scarce.

A genetic assessment of the genets (Genetta) and their relatives including Osbornictis was done by Gaubert et. al. in 2004 which demonstrated that Osbornictis is not a distinct lineage from Genetta and should thus be considered a junior synonym of Genetta.

As of April 25, 2020 the ITIS also lists Osbornictis [TSN 621834] as a junior synonym of Genetta [Cuvier, 1816; TSN 621833].

Osbornictis piscivora

Described as a new species of Viverridae by J. A. Allen on page 25.

Not much is known about this species—currently referred to as ‘Aquatic Genet’ as an English common name—not very many specimens have been collected. The species is still considered valid and is referenced in modern literature.

The genus is now considered to be a junior synonym of Genetta making the currently accepted nomenclature Genetta piscivora.

As of April 25, 2020 the ITIS lists Osbornictis piscivora [Allen, 1919; TSN 621998] as invalid, preferring the genus update to the nomenclature as Genetta piscivora [Allen, 1919; TSN 726261].

Sciurus niger avicennia

Described as a new subspecies of Sciurus niger [Linnæus, 1758] by Arthur H. Howell on page 37.

Unfortunately relevant recent academic papers that look at the phylogenetic relationships within Sciurus niger are behind paywalls and are very expensive to access, I was not able to look at them.

The ITIS as of April 30, 2020 however does recognize this subspecies as valid with the nomenclature of Sciurus niger avicennia [Howell, 1919; TSN 931247].

Xenogale

Described as a new genus of Viverridae by J. A. Allen on page 26 where he notes several resemblances to Herpestes, Atilax, and Ichneumia.

The genus was described for the purpose of a new species he was describing, Xenogale microdon.

Haymen (in Sanderson 1940) very convincingly demonstrated that the type species Xenogale microdon is synonymous with Herpestes naso [de Winton, 1901].

With the rules of taxonomical nomenclature, the type species junior synonomy to a previously described species must result in one of two things:

  1. The genus Xenogale is invalidated as a junior synonym of Herpestes.
  2. The species Herpestes naso becomes the type for Xenogale and thus becomes Xenogale naso.

Rosevear (1974, pages 329–336), Ansell (1978 citation needed), and Colyn and Van Rompaey (1994) chose the latter, keeping Xenogale as a monotypic genus containing only Xenogale naso.

Most mammalogists however it seems currently choose the former and treat Xenogale as a junior synonym to Herpestes. I literally searched online for hours looking for scholarly justification in treating Xenogale as a junior synonym to Herpestes. I found several expressed opinions that keeping Xenogale as valid would result in Herpestes being paraphyletic but I did not find even one peer reviewed journal article that backed up that opinion.

Opinions and peer reviewed science are not the same thing, even when those opinions are from scientists. Honestly I suspect (and note this suspicion is also just an opinion…) that if Xenogale microdon had been described before Herpestes naso so that it was the senior synonym, the genus Xenogale would still be considered valid and used by the majority of mammalogists writing papers on the species.

Patau et. al. (2009 figures 1–2) show that even without Xenogale, Herpestes already has major paraphyletic issues. It is very interesting to me that in their phylogenetic tree, Herpestes naso aligns with Atilax paludinosus to form a clade. This is interesting to me because Allen on page 27 notes that Xenogale was mistaken in the field for Atilax.

Parau et. al. in their discussion (l. c. page 75) do note that the closest relative to H. naso in their analysis is A. paludinosus but also note that in addition to some morphological differences, there are ecological differences and a very distant most recent common ancestor. They do not recommend placing the two species within the same genus.

They note that more research is needed, but if a taxonomic revision of Herpestes does result in something similar to their phylogenetic tree shown in figure 2 with H. naso and A. paludinosus forming a clade to the exclusion of other Herpestes then it seems more likely to me that Xenogale would be revived as a monotypic genus than for H. naso than to be placed within Atilax.

Xenogale Summary

It is very clear that the genus Herpestes is in need of taxonomic revision.

When that revision takes place, it is my suspicion the result will be very similar to Patau et. al. figure 2, with H. naso and A. paludinosus forming a monophyletic clade within the Herpestidae. It is further my suspicion that Xenogale will then be revived according to the rules of taxonomic nomenclature as a monotypic genus containing the species Xenogale naso, vindicating Allen and the minority of authors who continued to treat Xenogale as a valid genus even after Xenogale microdon was shown to be synonymous with Herpestes naso.

However, until that revision has been done and passed academic review, Xenogale should probably be treated as an invalid junior synonym of Herpestes [Illiger, 1811; TSN 180612] simply because that is the current consensus even if possibly a misguided consensus.

Xenogale microdon

Described as a new species of Viverridae by J. A. Allen on page 27.

As mentioned in the discussion for the genus, this taxon is now considered to be a junior synonym of Herpestes naso.

This does not however mean the description is invalid.

If it can be shown that the type specimen for Xenogale microdon differs enough from the type specimen for Herpestes naso to warrant subspecies differentiaton, then the description for Xenogale microdon would survive but as Herpestes naso microdon.

I was able to locate two authors who held this view.

Coetzee (in Meester and Setzer 1977 page ???) and Kingdon (1988 page 183) both recognized the trinomial nomenclature Herpestes naso microdon. Their support appears to be a minority view.

On the other hand, Colyn and Van Rompaey (1994) make a very good argument for treating it as a monotypic species with very compelling evidence that the three defined subspecies Xenogale [= Herpestes] naso naso, X. n. almodovari, and X. n. microdon can not be distinguished cranially. As a monotypic species, the description by J. A. Allen would be invalidated as a junior synonym.

The vast majority of authors seem to be in agreement with Colyn and Van Rompaey.

The overwhelming current consensus is that subspecies are not justified and that Xenogale microdon is an invalid junior synonym of Herpestes naso [de Winton, 1901 TSN 621894].
